Iterative Man

Origins

April 24, 2018 by Tom

"Smart people should build things." - Andrew Yang

I'd been working in the insurance industry for a little over a decade, but something had changed. I was a successful Process Consultant/Project Manager with a great salary, tons of vacation time, and the ability to work from home. So why wasn't I happy? The things I enjoyed about my work became fewer and farther between. I was good at my job, but the passion was fading. Sound familiar?

Walking away from a comfortable job that afforded me plenty of autonomy and opportunities to engage with senior leaders in my company seemed crazy. But as 2017 drew to a close, it felt like the walls were closing in. I couldn't seem to muster the energy to go the extra mile like I had in years past. Basic tasks started to feel mundane and unworthy of the effort. I knew that I couldn't spend another year drifting along. I was too young, too determined to make a mark, but it was clear that I'd have to make a serious pivot to find my true calling.

Web Development

It started almost as an afterthought. I'd been looking at side gigs that could bring in extra money and building websites on the side sounded like it could be fun. As a practitioner of multiple martial arts, I thought I could specialize in updating and improving the typically awful sites that I saw for most of the schools in my area. So I signed up for a two month CodeAcademy intensive course on "Building Web Sites from Scratch."

I learned some HTML, some CSS, and put together a few project sites. And something inside, just clicked. Even though it wasn't "real" programming, I felt at home in arranging the elements and customizing them just so. And it got me wondering "How deep does this rabbit hole go?"

When Conditions Are Right, Things Manifest

I had a meeting with my boss on the morning of January 2nd. Our weekly chats were normally full of project discussion and reviewing plans, but this one took a turn rather quickly. She asked "Do you still enjoy this job?" Maybe it was the fact that I hadn't slept well the night before, but something inside me screamed, "Just tell her!"

So I did. It all poured out: the frustration, the sadness, the guilt, and the NEED for something new. And the most incredible part - she wanted to help. So I had the runway I needed to make my exit plan. But, what exactly was the plan??? In my next post, I'll talk about my research into the various options for my transition and how I landed on Skill Distillery in Denver as my best move.
